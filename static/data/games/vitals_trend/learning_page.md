# Vitals Trend Spotter

Trend recognition is different from single-vital memorization. One set of vitals can look acceptable; the direction of change tells you whether the patient is compensating, tiring, or decompensating.

## What To Watch

- Rising heart rate plus falling blood pressure suggests perfusion is worsening.
- Falling respiratory rate is not always improvement; if oxygen saturation is also falling, think fatigue.
- Pediatric patients can compensate until they suddenly cannot. Trend changes often matter before one isolated number looks dramatic.

## Field Traps

- Do not call a falling respiratory rate "better" without checking oxygenation and work of breathing.
- Do not wait for severe hypotension before treating pediatric shock.
- Do not anchor on the first normal SpO2 if later values are falling.

## Field Examples

- Asthma: RR drops from 46 to 24 while SpO2 falls from 91% to 83%. This is fatigue, not improvement.
- Sepsis: HR and RR climb while systolic pressure trends down. Treat the trend as perfusion failure.
- Dehydration: tachycardia with falling BP after vomiting/diarrhea is hypovolemic shock until proven otherwise.

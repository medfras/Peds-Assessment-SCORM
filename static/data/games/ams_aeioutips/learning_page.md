# AMS Differential: AEIOUTIPS

Use **AEIOUTIPS** as a systematic mnemonic to avoid anchoring on a single AMS cause. Most findings have multiple plausible causes — your job is to recognize them all and keep a broad differential until the clinical picture narrows.

## A — Alcohol / Acidosis

- Acute intoxication causes CNS depression, ataxia, slurred speech, and disinhibition.
- Alcohol withdrawal (6–48 h after last drink) produces autonomic hyperactivity: diaphoresis, tremor, tachycardia, hypertension, and seizures.
- Alcoholic ketoacidosis causes a high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis with Kussmaul respirations.
- Any severe metabolic acidosis — regardless of cause — can produce or worsen AMS through CNS toxicity and poor cerebral perfusion.

## E — Epilepsy / Electrolytes

- Postictal confusion after a generalized seizure can last minutes to hours — ask bystanders about any witnessed convulsive activity.
- Absence seizures and complex partial seizures produce AMS without tonic-clonic movement.
- Severe hyponatremia (low sodium) is the most common electrolyte cause of acute AMS and seizure in the prehospital setting.
- Hypomagnesemia and hypocalcemia lower the seizure threshold and may cause tetany or AMS.
- Hypernatremia causes brain cell shrinkage and progressive confusion.

## I — Insulin / Infection

- Hypoglycemia is the most immediately life-threatening reversible cause of AMS — check glucose on every altered patient.
- Signs: diaphoresis, tremor, tachycardia, pallor, agitation, progressing to stupor and seizure if untreated.
- Hyperglycemia in DKA produces fruity breath, Kussmaul respirations, dehydration, and gradual-onset AMS.
- Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS) causes profound dehydration and AMS at extreme glucose levels without acidosis.
- Sepsis and meningitis cause AMS through inflammatory cytokines, hemodynamic compromise, and direct CNS invasion.
- Any fever with altered mentation should prompt consideration of CNS infection (meningitis, encephalitis) until ruled out.

## O — Overdose / Oxygen

- Opioids: pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression, decreased LOC — responsive to naloxone.
- CNS depressants (benzos, barbiturates, alcohol): slurred speech, ataxia, sedation.
- Stimulants (cocaine, amphetamines): agitation, dilated pupils, tachycardia, hyperthermia.
- Anticholinergics: dry, flushed, dilated pupils, tachycardia, urinary retention, confusion ("Mad as a hatter, red as a beet…").
- Cholinergics / organophosphates: SLUDGE + DUMBELS, pinpoint pupils, secretions, bradycardia.
- Hypoxia impairs every neurological function — AMS from hypoxia can mimic any other category. Rule it out early with SpO₂ and clinical exam.
- CO poisoning causes AMS without cyanosis — SpO₂ is falsely normal. Consider with enclosed space exposure.

## U — Uremia

- Renal failure causes accumulation of nitrogenous waste products that are directly neurotoxic.
- Signs: ammonia or urine-like breath (uremic fetor), uremic frost (white deposits on skin), fatigue, confusion, and eventually coma.
- Often accompanies other chronic disease — common co-finding with hypertension, diabetes, and heart failure.

## T — Trauma / Temperature / Toxins

- Head trauma: immediate or delayed AMS from TBI, intracranial hemorrhage, or cerebral contusion.
- Always ask about a mechanism even if not volunteered — a fall may precede or result from another cause.
- Hypothermia: impairs cardiac conduction and CNS function; alcohol, opioids, and cold environments are common contributors.
- Hyperthermia (heat stroke, malignant hyperthermia, NMS): core temp above 40°C with CNS dysfunction is a true emergency.
- Environmental and ingested toxins: carbon monoxide, heavy metals, cyanide, and plant toxins can all present as AMS without a clear drug history.

## P — Psychiatric

- Psychosis, acute mania, and severe depression can all produce behavioral changes that appear as AMS.
- Psychiatric AMS is a diagnosis of exclusion in the prehospital setting — medical causes must be ruled out first.
- Agitation and combativeness are never safely attributed to psychiatric cause until hypoxia, hypoglycemia, and toxicology are cleared.

## S — Stroke / Shock / Seizure (Postictal)

- Ischemic stroke: sudden onset focal deficits, FAST findings, may include AMS if large-vessel or posterior circulation.
- Hemorrhagic stroke: often more severe and faster onset; may include the "worst headache of my life."
- Shock: hypovolemic, cardiogenic, distributive, or obstructive — all reduce cerebral perfusion and cause AMS.
- Septic shock is a combined infectious and hemodynamic cause.
- Postictal state: the period of CNS recovery after a seizure; the patient may be deeply confused, combative, or unresponsive for up to an hour.

---

## Key Principles

- **Multiple causes can be present simultaneously.** Hypoglycemia can trigger a seizure. Trauma can cause hemorrhage and hypoxia. Sepsis causes shock and metabolic derangement.
- **Treat the reversible causes first.** Glucose, oxygen, and naloxone have excellent safety profiles and rapid effect — do not withhold while pursuing a diagnosis.
- **Hypoxia is always on the differential.** Never anchor until SpO₂, work of breathing, and airway are confirmed adequate.
- **Behavior is not a diagnosis.** Combativeness, paranoia, and agitation require ruling out medical causes before attributing to psychiatric etiology.

# Adult vs. Child A&P

Classify anatomy and physiology facts as adult, child, or shared.

## Airway

- Children have proportionally larger tongues and smaller airways, so small amounts of swelling or secretions can cause significant obstruction.
- The pediatric occiput is proportionally larger, which can flex the neck when supine; neutral positioning may require shoulder padding.
- Infants are obligate nose breathers, so nasal congestion can meaningfully increase work of breathing.

## Breathing

- Children rely heavily on respiratory rate to maintain minute ventilation.
- Chest walls are more compliant, so retractions can be prominent when work of breathing rises.
- Respiratory failure is a common pathway to pediatric arrest.

## Circulation

- Children can maintain blood pressure through tachycardia and vasoconstriction until late shock.
- Hypotension is a late and ominous pediatric finding.
- Capillary refill, skin signs, mental status, pulse quality, and heart rate trends matter before the blood pressure drops.

## Proportions and Heat

- Children have larger heads relative to body size and a higher surface-area-to-mass ratio.
- They lose heat faster than adults and can become hypothermic during exposure, trauma care, or wet clothing removal.
- Head injuries and airway positioning are influenced by pediatric proportions.

## Other Pediatric Considerations

- Medication dosing is weight-based, so estimated weight and dose checks are safety-critical.
- Communication must match developmental stage; use caregivers as historians without ignoring the child.
- Normal vital signs vary by age, so compare findings against age-appropriate ranges.

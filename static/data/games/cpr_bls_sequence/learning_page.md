# BLS CPR: Chain of Survival

The BLS sequence exists to remove delay. The faster a team recognizes arrest, starts high-quality CPR, applies an AED, and restarts compressions after each analysis, the more likely the patient is to survive with neurologic function.

## Out-of-Hospital Chain of Survival

1. Recognize cardiac arrest and activate the emergency response system.
2. Begin immediate high-quality CPR.
3. Defibrillate rapidly when a shockable rhythm is present.
4. Provide advanced resuscitation by EMS.
5. Continue post-cardiac arrest care after ROSC.
6. Support recovery.

## Recognition

Start CPR when the patient is unresponsive and has absent or abnormal breathing. Agonal gasps are not normal breathing. Limit pulse checks to 10 seconds; if you are unsure, start compressions.

## Sequence Discipline

1. Confirm unresponsiveness and abnormal or absent breathing.
2. Activate help and request AED/monitor.
3. Begin compressions immediately.
4. Apply AED pads while compressions continue.
5. Pause only for analysis and shock/no-shock decision.
6. Resume compressions immediately after the decision.
7. Rotate compressors about every 2 minutes or sooner if quality drops.

## Adult, Child, and Infant Differences

| Step | Adult | Child | Infant |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pulse site | Carotid | Carotid or femoral | Brachial |
| Single rescuer ratio | 30:2 | 30:2 | 30:2 |
| Two rescuer ratio before advanced airway | 30:2 | 15:2 | 15:2 |
| Compression depth | At least 2 in / 5 cm | About 2 in / 5 cm, at least 1/3 AP diameter | About 1.5 in / 4 cm, at least 1/3 AP diameter |
| Preferred two-rescuer infant technique | Not applicable | Not applicable | Two-thumb encircling technique |
| Advanced airway ventilation | 1 breath every 6 sec | 1 breath every 2-3 sec | 1 breath every 2-3 sec |

Pediatric arrests are often respiratory in origin, so ventilations matter. Do not let that become an excuse for long pauses; ventilate efficiently and keep compressions moving.

## AED and Rhythm Check Rules

- Apply pads during compressions.
- Stop compressions only when the device analyzes or the team is ready for the rhythm decision.
- During AED analysis, check a central pulse for 5-10 seconds so rhythm and pulse assessment happen in the same pause.
- Keep the total interruption under 10 seconds whenever possible.
- Shock shockable rhythms, then immediately resume CPR.
- Do not perform routine post-shock pulse checks.
- Reassess rhythm after the next CPR cycle unless ROSC signs appear.

## High-Performance Team Behaviors

- Compressor announces fatigue early and rotates cleanly.
- Airway gives visible chest rise without forceful or rapid ventilation.
- AED/monitor operator prepares the next analysis before the pause.
- Team lead counts down the pause and calls "resume CPR" immediately.
- Everyone knows the next job before compressions stop.

## Reversible Causes: Hs and Ts

Keep the CPR sequence moving while the team searches for treatable causes of arrest.

**Hs:** hypovolemia, hypoxia, hydrogen ion/acidosis, hypo-/hyperkalemia or other metabolic derangement, hypothermia.

**Ts:** tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade, toxins, thrombosis-pulmonary embolism, thrombosis-coronary/MI.

Use the scene, history, rhythm, ETCO2, lung sounds, trauma findings, glucose/metabolic clues, and treatment response to decide which reversible cause is most likely.

The sequence game tests ordering. The CPR scenario tests execution. A good sequence should feel calm, fast, and predictable.

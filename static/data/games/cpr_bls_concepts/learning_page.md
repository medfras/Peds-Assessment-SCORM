# CPR Mastery: AHA BLS Quality Targets

High-quality CPR is not just "doing compressions." It is a set of measurable behaviors that protect coronary and brain perfusion until defibrillation, ROSC, or higher-level care can take over.

The drill focuses on the targets EMS crews can control: compression quality, pause discipline, ventilation discipline, AED timing, and team choreography.

## Core AHA BLS Targets

| Skill | Adult | Child | Infant |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compression rate | 100-120/min | 100-120/min | 100-120/min |
| Compression depth | At least 2 in / 5 cm; avoid more than 2.4 in / 6 cm | At least 1/3 AP chest diameter, about 2 in / 5 cm | At least 1/3 AP chest diameter, about 1.5 in / 4 cm |
| Recoil | Full chest recoil; do not lean | Full chest recoil | Full chest recoil |
| Single rescuer ratio | 30:2 | 30:2 | 30:2 |
| Two rescuer ratio, no advanced airway | 30:2 | 15:2 | 15:2 |
| Advanced airway ventilation | 1 breath every 6 seconds, about 10/min | 1 breath every 2-3 seconds, about 20-30/min | 1 breath every 2-3 seconds, about 20-30/min |

## High-Performance CPR Priorities

**Chest compression fraction (CCF): greater than 80%.** CCF is the percentage of arrest time with compressions actually in progress. High-performance teams protect CCF by applying pads during compressions, pre-charging when appropriate, rotating compressors quickly, and restarting immediately after shock or no-shock decisions.

**Pause discipline: less than 10 seconds.** Rhythm analysis, shock delivery, pulse checks, airway attempts, and compressor swaps all create hands-off time. During AED analysis, use the rhythm-check pause to check a central pulse for 5-10 seconds. The goal is not just "short pauses"; it is planned pauses with a clear job and an immediate restart.

**Depth and recoil: both matter.** Shallow compressions fail to generate enough forward flow. Leaning prevents venous return and reduces the output of the next compression. Quality CPR needs both a firm push and a full release.

**Rate: stay in the window.** Slower than 100/min reduces delivered compressions. Faster than 120/min commonly becomes shallow and reduces filling time. Speed without depth and recoil is not high-quality CPR.

**Ventilation: enough, not too much.** Hyperventilation raises intrathoracic pressure, reduces venous return, and can worsen perfusion during CPR. After an advanced airway, do not pause compressions for breaths; ventilate asynchronously at the age-appropriate rate.

## Team Roles During a Code

**Compressor:** Delivers 100-120/min, correct depth, full recoil, and calls fatigue early.

**Airway:** Opens the airway, seals the BVM, gives controlled breaths, and avoids over-ventilation.

**AED/monitor:** Places pads during compressions, announces analysis, minimizes the pause, and calls immediate restart.

**Team lead:** Assigns roles, watches time, controls rhythm checks, prevents unnecessary pulse checks, and keeps the next action ready before the pause starts.

## Common Field Errors

- Checking a pulse after every shock instead of restarting CPR.
- Stopping compressions to place AED pads.
- Letting airway attempts interrupt compressions too long.
- Compressing too fast and too shallow.
- Leaning on the chest between compressions.
- Continuing 30:2 after an advanced airway is placed.
- Using the adult post-airway ventilation rate for infants and children.

## Reversible Causes: Hs and Ts

Cardiac arrest management is not only compressions and shocks. While CPR continues, the team should look for reversible causes suggested by the rhythm, scene, history, exam, ETCO2, and response to treatment.

**Hs**

- **Hypovolemia:** severe bleeding, dehydration, fluid loss, or distributive shock with poor preload.
- **Hypoxia:** airway failure, respiratory arrest, drowning, choking, severe asthma, anaphylaxis, or inadequate ventilation.
- **Hydrogen ion / acidosis:** prolonged downtime, poor ventilation, sepsis, DKA, or severe shock.
- **Hypo-/hyperkalemia or metabolic derangement:** renal failure, dialysis patient, DKA, crush injury, medication effects.
- **Hypothermia:** environmental exposure, submersion, trauma exposure, or cold stress.

**Ts**

- **Tension pneumothorax:** trauma, positive-pressure ventilation, unilateral breath sounds, obstructive shock signs.
- **Tamponade, cardiac:** penetrating trauma, muffled heart tones, JVD, narrowing pulse pressure when present.
- **Toxins:** opioid, tricyclic, beta blocker, calcium channel blocker, sodium-channel blocker, or other overdose patterns.
- **Thrombosis, pulmonary:** PE risk, sudden collapse, hypoxia, right-heart strain clues.
- **Thrombosis, coronary:** suspected MI, chest pain before arrest, shockable rhythm, cardiac history.

## What This Drill Is Testing

The quiz asks you to choose the right CPR action for the patient, rescuer count, airway status, rhythm/shock moment, and quality target. To unlock the CPR scenario phase, score at least 4 out of 5.

The scenario phase then tests whether those numbers become behavior: start compressions quickly, keep pauses short, use the AED correctly, check pulse only when appropriate, and maintain CPR quality until the code ends.

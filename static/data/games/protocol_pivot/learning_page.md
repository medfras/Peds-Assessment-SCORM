# Protocol Pivot

Protocol Pivot trains the single most critical and under-practiced skill in EMS: **updating your clinical model mid-call when new information contradicts your initial impression.**

## The Two Acts

**Act 1 — Initial Impression**
Form a working diagnosis from the presenting information and identify the highest-priority action. This is standard clinical reasoning — you treat the presentation in front of you.

**Act 2 — The Pivot**
New information arrives during the call: a vital sign changes, a bystander reveals new history, a clinical sign appears, or a treatment fails to work as expected. You must now decide: does your initial impression hold, or do you need to change course?

## Why Anchoring Kills

**Anchoring** is the cognitive error of holding your initial diagnosis even when contradictory evidence accumulates. It is one of the most common causes of prehospital treatment error:

- The patient with "COPD exacerbation" who develops crackles and an S3 — and gets another bronchodilator instead of CPAP
- The "ACS" patient whose pain tears through to the back with a bilateral BP discrepancy — and gets aspirin
- The "febrile seizure" child who develops a petechial rash — and gets transported at routine priority

The initial impression was not wrong. **Failing to update it when the evidence changed was.**

## The Pivot Pattern

When new information arrives, ask three questions:
1. Does this finding fit my current diagnosis?
2. What diagnosis would explain both the initial presentation AND this new finding?
3. What changes in my treatment or transport if the new diagnosis is correct?

## Scoring

- **Act 1 correct: 1 point** — You identified the appropriate initial management.
- **Act 2 correct: 2 points** — You updated your clinical model when new evidence required it. This is the CE skill.

The scoring reflects reality: the initial impression is easier and worth less. The pivot under pressure — especially when it contradicts your own earlier decision — is where clinical expertise lives.

## Anchoring Trap

In every Protocol Pivot case, one Act 2 wrong answer is tagged as the **anchoring trap**: the choice that repeats the Act 1 treatment despite the pivot finding. This is the most common real-world error. It is always wrong in Act 2 — and always tempting.

# Respiratory Differential Discriminators

Use this reference to identify the single finding that separates each respiratory emergency from the others. Every field diagnosis starts with the same undifferentiated presentation — wheeze, stridor, or distress. The discriminating finding is what drives protocol selection.

---

## The Five Differentials

### Asthma / Bronchospasm
**Key discriminating clue:** Known reactive airway disease history with bilateral expiratory wheeze and prolonged expiratory phase. Symptoms are bilateral and diffuse — never unilateral.

**First EMS action:** Bronchodilator therapy. Albuterol via nebulizer or MDI with spacer. Reassess air entry and work of breathing frequently. Contact medical direction for persistent or severe exacerbations.

---

### Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)
**Key discriminating clue:** Viral URI prodrome (runny nose, low-grade fever for 1–5 days) followed by sudden barking seal-like cough and inspiratory stridor, typically worse at night and with crying. Affects toddlers most commonly.

**First EMS action:** Keep the child calm — agitation dramatically worsens stridor. Position of comfort in caregiver's arms. High-flow oxygen as tolerated. Minimize procedures. Transport per upper-airway obstruction protocol.

---

### Epiglottitis (Supraglottic Infection)
**Key discriminating clue:** Tripod positioning (leaning forward with arms braced), drooling (cannot swallow secretions), muffled "hot potato" voice, and toxic quiet appearance. The child is still and terrified — not crying, not barking.

**First EMS action:** Do NOT examine the throat. Do NOT agitate the child. Do NOT force supine positioning. Position of comfort, high-flow O2 via blow-by, immediate transport with ALS backup. Airway can close rapidly.

---

### Anaphylaxis
**Key discriminating clue:** Known or suspected antigen exposure (food, insect sting, medication) followed rapidly by multi-system involvement — any combination of airway (wheeze, throat tightness, stridor), skin (urticaria, angioedema, flushing), and circulation (hypotension). Skin findings are absent in some cases — the onset story may be the only discriminating feature.

**First EMS action:** Epinephrine IM (mid-outer thigh) is the first-line treatment. Do not substitute albuterol alone — bronchospasm in anaphylaxis is driven by allergic mediators that epinephrine directly counters. IV access, position supine (unless airway compromise), high-flow O2, transport.

---

### Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)
**Key discriminating clue:** Sudden onset with no prodrome, occurring during eating or play with small objects. Completely asymptomatic immediately before. Unilateral decreased air entry or wheeze (most commonly right-sided, as the right mainstem bronchus is straighter and wider). No fever, no URI, no allergen exposure.

**First EMS action:** If cough is effective — encourage forceful coughing, do not interfere. If cough is ineffective or patient is deteriorating — follow FBAO protocol (back blows and chest thrusts for infants; abdominal thrusts for children over 1 year). If patient loses consciousness, begin CPR and visualize airway.

## Common Anchoring Traps

**Wheeze does not equal asthma.** Wheeze is a sound, not a diagnosis. Anaphylaxis causes bronchospasm with wheeze. FBAO can cause unilateral wheeze. Croup can transmit upper-airway sounds to the chest. Ask: is the wheeze bilateral and diffuse (lower-airway disease), or unilateral (obstruction), or part of a multi-system picture (allergic)?

**Stridor does not always mean croup.** Epiglottitis causes inspiratory stridor too. The distinction is the child's behavior and appearance — a crying, barking child is more consistent with croup; a silent, drooling, forward-leaning toxic child is epiglottitis.

**Fever does not distinguish epiglottitis from croup.** Both can present with elevated temperature. The discriminating feature is appearance: consolable and crying vs. toxic and still.

**Onset story often outweighs auscultation.** For FBAO and anaphylaxis in particular, what happened immediately before the symptoms matters more than what you hear on exam. Always ask the 30-second history before picking up your stethoscope.

**Absence of skin findings does not exclude anaphylaxis.** Urticaria and angioedema appear in many cases but are not required for the diagnosis. A patient with allergen exposure, throat tightness, and hypotension has anaphylaxis regardless of whether hives are present.

# DMIST Handoff Builder

DMIST is the structured radio and verbal handoff format for EMS-to-hospital communication. A well-built DMIST handoff gives the receiving team a complete clinical picture in 60–90 seconds — and omits everything they cannot act on.

## The Five Priority Bands

| Band | Content | Examples |
|------|---------|---------|
| 1 – Demographics / Mechanism | Who + what happened | Age, sex, chief complaint, mechanism of injury, time of onset |
| 2 – Key Findings | What you found | Relevant vitals, exam findings, pertinent history |
| 3 – Interventions | What you did | Medications given, procedures performed, airway interventions |
| 4 – Response / Trends | How the patient responded | Directional vital sign changes, pain response, mental status trend |
| 5 – Transport / ETA | Where + when | Estimated arrival, specific destination request, pre-notifications (cath lab, trauma team) |

## What to Leave Out

An effective handoff is **clinically dense, not socially dense**. The receiving team cannot act on:

- Social circumstances (living situation, family dynamics, employment)
- Legal matters (warrants, outstanding tickets, liability)
- Property damage or financial details
- Bystander opinions and speculation
- Mechanism details already captured in band 1 (do not repeat)

Irrelevant information does not fill the handoff — it crowds out information that matters.

## Why Sequence Matters

The receiving team begins forming their clinical impression with the first words out of your mouth. Starting with demographics and mechanism frames everything that follows. Interventions placed before findings leave the team wondering what prompted the treatment. Response/trends last — they confirm whether the treatment worked, and that context only lands once the rest of the picture is established.

## Handoff Quality in EMS

Research consistently shows that EMS-to-ED handoffs suffer most from:
1. **Omission** — Required information not transmitted at all
2. **Sequence errors** — Treatments mentioned before findings, making the logic unclear
3. **Noise** — Non-clinical detail that interrupts the receiving team's absorption

Practicing with DMIST Builder builds the habit of mentally sorting clinical information by band before you pick up the radio.

## Radio vs. Bedside Handoffs

Radio handoff (en route): brief, bands 1–2 + destination/ETA, cath lab or trauma activation if needed.

Bedside handoff (on arrival): full DMIST, with band 4 response/trends emphasized because the receiving team can now observe the patient directly.
